Geospatial modeling of preterm birth risk across U.S. counties.

Across 570 U.S. counties with populations above 100,000, socioeconomic vulnerability, healthcare access, and maternal demographics jointly shape preterm birth outcomes. This analysis benchmarks nine modeling paradigms — from ordinary least squares to deep neural networks to Geographically Weighted Regression — and asks where each approach succeeds, where it fails, and what the residuals reveal.

§ 02 · Model Performance

Eight models, one spatial winner.

Comparison of parametric, ensemble machine-learning, deep learning, and spatial-regression approaches. Tree-based ensembles (XGBoost, Gradient Boosting) capture non-linearities that OLS misses, but their improvements are modest relative to what Geographically Weighted Regression achieves by letting coefficients vary across space. Full-sample R² is reported since GWR does not use train/test splits.

On metric choice: ML and NN models are reported at full-sample R² for comparability with GWR, which has no natural train/test split. Test-set R² values (where applicable) range from 0.499 (Random Forest) to 0.533 (Stacking Ensemble), confirming that generalization gains from spatial structure exceed those from model complexity alone.
§ 03 · Spatial Autocorrelation Diagnostics

Global models leave spatial signal on the table.

Moran's I quantifies how much of the residual variation clusters geographically rather than scattering randomly. A well-specified model should yield residuals with I ≈ 0. The raw outcome variable shows moderate spatial clustering (I = 0.162, p < 0.001). OLS residuals retain structure (I = 0.075, p < 0.001), indicating omitted geographic processes. GWR residuals approach spatial independence (I = 0.006, p = 0.22), validating the local-coefficient specification.

§ 04 · Residual Geography

The visible signature of model misspecification.

OLS residuals (left) cluster systematically — the global model consistently under-predicts in the Southeast and over-predicts in much of the Mountain West and New England. GWR residuals (right) scatter without geographic structure. This is the visual analog of the Moran's I reduction: what global coefficients mask, local coefficients absorb.

§ 06 · Local Model Fit

Where the model sees clearly, and where it doesn't.

GWR computes a distinct R² for every county using only that county's local neighborhood. High local R² values indicate geographies where the seven predictors cleanly explain preterm birth variation; low values mark regions where unmodeled factors — provider density, environmental exposures, health-system characteristics — likely drive outcomes beyond what the SDOH framework captures. This map points to where future research should focus.

§ 07 · Predictor Importance

Which variables the ensemble leans on.

Permutation feature importance from the tuned Random Forest, measured on the held-out test set. Values represent mean decrease in R² when a predictor is randomly shuffled — higher values indicate the model depends more heavily on that variable. Percentage Black population and median household income together account for the overwhelming share of predictive power, consistent with the strong bivariate correlations observed in Module 3 (r = +0.530 and r = −0.470 respectively).

On methodology

This dashboard accompanies a three-phase spatial epidemiology analysis of county-level preterm birth rates across the contiguous United States for calendar year 2023. The framework integrates CDC WONDER natality data with the American Community Survey and Census Gazetteer coordinates, benchmarking nine modeling approaches against Geographically Weighted Regression to test for spatial non-stationarity in the social determinants of preterm birth. All dashboard values — model predictions, residuals, local coefficients, and local R² — are fitted outputs from the Phase 2B notebook, not simulated.
